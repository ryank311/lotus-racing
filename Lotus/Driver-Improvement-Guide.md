## 1. Trail Braking in a Mid-Engine Car

### Why You're Getting Oversteer

Your oversteer on trail-brake is likely caused by one of:
- Keeping brake pressure on too long past turn-in (overloads front, unweights rear)
- Releasing brake pressure too abruptly (sudden weight shift destabilizes the rear)
- Too much trail-brake pressure for the corner speed

The Exige's stock brake bias is heavily front-weighted. Even light trail braking produces significant front-end grip and rear unloading. **You need less trail brake pressure than you think.**

### Technique for the Exige

1. **Brake hard in a straight line first.** Get the majority of deceleration done before turn-in
2. **Trail only lightly past turn-in.** The brake-to-cornering transition should be a smooth, linear taper -- not an on/off switch
3. **Consider trailing throttle as a complement.** Engine braking in a low gear shifts weight forward effectively and keeps your foot closer to the throttle for quicker recovery if the rear steps out
4. **The transition from brake to throttle is everything.** More braking = more front grip / less rear grip. Your job is to smoothly taper brake pressure as you add steering lock

### The Brake Release Drill (Ross Bentley)

Spend 1-2 laps each with:
1. **Early, slow release** of brakes past turn-in
2. **Early, fast release**
3. **Late, slow release**
4. **Late, fast release**

After each pair of laps, note: "when I do *this*, the car does *that*." You're building a mental matrix of brake-release responses specific to your car.

### Progressive Trail Braking Build-Up (Blayze)

1. Start by braking conservatively and **coasting** into corners (no trail brake at all)
2. Gradually carry 5-10% brake pressure past turn-in on successive laps
3. Slowly increase amount and duration of trail braking as you build feel
4. Goal: a smooth brake release synchronized with steering input

## 2. Braking Zone Optimization

### Common Intermediate Mistakes at VIR

From Race & Track Driving's VIR data analysis:

- **Lifting before the brake point.** On the main straight, many drivers feather throttle before the actual braking marker. Stay at 100% throttle until the actual brake point -- this alone can gain 1-2 car lengths of speed
- **Panic-braking when you brake late.** Data shows that braking *later* sometimes produces *lower* turn-in speed because the driver brakes too aggressively. Fix: brake slightly earlier but with confidence, carry more speed to the turn-in point
- **Inconsistent braking references.** You need a visual braking marker for every corner. Hit it within one car length for 5+ laps before moving it later

### Finding the Limit Progressively

1. Brake at a comfortable point
2. Hit that point +/- 1 car length for 5 consecutive laps
3. Move it later by one car length
4. Repeat

**The Exige's slip angle is narrow.** Once you exceed the rear tires' grip, the car transitions from grip to spin very quickly. A7s provide less warning than street compounds. Respect this and build braking confidence gradually.

## 3. Slow Corner Technique

### Exit Speed vs. Minimum Speed

**For slow corners onto straights (Oak Tree, Hog Pen):** Exit speed wins decisively. A 2 mph higher exit speed at Oak Tree compounds across the entire 4,000-foot back straight.

**The sequence:**
1. First, maximize exit speed: late apex, rotate the car early, get on throttle as early as possible
2. Once exit speed is optimized, work on raising minimum corner speed *without* sacrificing exit
3. The minimum speed point should be near the apex -- if it's well before, you're over-slowing

**For linked corners (Snake, Esses):** Minimum speed through the complex matters more since there's no long straight after. Maintain momentum and flow.

### Throttle Application in the Exige

- **Never abruptly lift mid-corner.** This is the #1 cause of incidents in mid-engine cars. The weight transfer forward is sudden and the rear breaks loose
- **Maintain "maintenance throttle" through the corner.** Even small throttle keeps weight on the rear. This is your safety net
- **Apply throttle like there's an egg under your foot.** The initial re-engagement from trail-brake to acceleration is the critical transition. If you feel a jolt, you were too aggressive
- **The Exige corners beautifully under power.** From mid-corner on, the game is putting down as much power as early as you can

### Vision

- **Always look two reference points ahead.** In the braking zone, look at the apex. At turn-in, look at the exit. At the apex, look down the straight
- **Fluid eye movement, not snapping.** Let your eyes trace the path you want the car to follow
- **In technical sequences (Snake, Esses):** Your eyes need to be ahead of the car's current position at all times

## 4. Using the Garmin Catalyst Effectively

### During Sessions (Real-Time)
- Follow audio cues one at a time: "brake later," "apex later," etc.
- Don't try to fix everything at once -- pick one corner or one technique per session

### Post-Session Review
- **Opportunities tab:** Shows your top 3 sectors with the most time to gain, with video comparison of your optimal vs. average
- **Braking tab:** Shows where you began braking on your best lap vs. average laps, with side-by-side images at the exact braking point. Use this to find visual reference markers on track
- **True Optimal Lap:** Splices your best sectors into a composite "what's possible" lap. This is your benchmark
- **Speed trace overlay:** Your best vs. average lap on speed-vs-distance. The gaps between the lines are your opportunities

### Key Metrics to Watch
- **Sector time variance:** Which sectors swing the most between your best and worst laps? Focus there first
- **Speed at braking points:** Are you reaching full speed before braking, or lifting early?
- **Minimum corner speed:** Compare your minimum speed through each corner vs. your best lap through that corner
- **Lateral G consistency:** Drops to low lateral G during transitions = "dead time" where the car is neither braking hard nor cornering hard

### Limitation
The Catalyst does not capture brake pressure, steering angle, or throttle position. It can miss some opportunities. Periodic check-ins with a human coach catch what the device misses.

## 5. Building Consistency

### Before Chasing Speed

If your laps range from 2:13 to 2:18, you're not ready to push for 2:10. Get your spread under 1 second first.

**How to build consistency:**
1. **Memorize reference points.** Braking marker, turn-in point, apex, track-out point for every corner. Hit them every lap
2. **Set process goals, not outcome goals.** Instead of "run 2:10," set "hit my Turn 1 braking marker within one car length for 10 consecutive laps"
3. **Warm up properly.** Don't push on lap 2. Take 2 laps to warm tires, brakes, and your brain
4. **The 80/20 rule:** Focus on the 20% of corners responsible for 80% of your time loss

### Pre-Session Mental Preparation
- **Visualize the lap.** Before each session, close your eyes and drive the lap mentally. Picture each braking zone, turn-in, apex, and exit
- **Pick one focus per session.** "This session I'm working on Oak Tree exit" or "This session I'm working on trail braking into Turn 1"
- **Debrief between sessions.** Take notes, review Catalyst data, discuss with other drivers
