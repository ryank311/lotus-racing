# VIR Full Course Driving Guide -- 2008 Exige Club Racer

Research compiled from racing forums, coaching platforms, track guides, and SCCA/NASA community data. Tailored to a ~2150 lb mid-engine Exige on Hoosier A7s with Nitron 3-way suspension, running 2:14-2:15s.

---

## 1. Braking Zone Optimization

### General Principles for the Exige at VIR

The Exige's light weight (sub-2200 lbs with driver) and mid-engine layout create specific braking characteristics at VIR:

- **Shorter braking distances than most track cars** -- You will out-brake heavier cars into every corner. The AP Racing front setup with XP10 pads gives strong initial bite. The danger is braking *too early* and losing time coasting.
- **Trail braking is your primary weapon** -- Mid-engine cars rotate beautifully under trail braking. The 40/60 weight distribution means the rear naturally wants to come around as you decelerate. Use this deliberately rather than fighting it.
- **The A7s provide less audible warning** -- You lose the tire squeal cues. Breakaway is less progressive than street compounds. This matters most in the braking zones where you are near the limit of longitudinal grip.

### Turn 1 (The Horseshoe) -- MAJOR BRAKING ZONE

**The braking zone is downhill and often slippery, with very little camber to help slow the car.** This is the most common place to over-brake at VIR.

- **Common mistake:** Lifting off throttle before the actual brake point on the front straight. Stay at full power until the actual braking marker. Many drivers give up 1-2 car lengths by feathering throttle at the end of the straight.
- **Common mistake:** Braking too hard too early, getting the car slowed down, then coasting through the turn. This is slow. You want continuous deceleration all the way to the apex.
- **Correct technique:** Brake hard initially, then progressively release pressure while turning in. Trail brake all the way to the apex (second half of inside curbing). The first half of Turn 1 is tighter than the second half (increasing radius), so if you slow enough for the tightest part, you can accelerate through the second half.
- **For the Exige specifically:** Trail braking is the faster line here vs. a late-apex/early-throttle approach. Your lightweight mid-engine car rotates well under trail braking. The late-apex line is better for high-HP cars that need early throttle -- your 218 hp doesn't need that.
- **Exit priority:** Get the car pointed and on throttle early. The run to Turn 2 is short but every tenth counts.

**Source:** [Race & Track Driving -- VIR Full](https://racetrackdriving.com/track-guide/vir-full/), [Blayze -- Top 3 Mistakes at VIR](https://blayze.io/blog/car-racing/top-3-mistakes-at-virginia-international-raceway)

### Turn 3 (NASCAR Bend) -- MODERATE BRAKING ZONE

- Decreasing radius corner. Easy to carry too much speed in and run wide at exit.
- The braking reference is the inside curb at Turn 2. Begin braking as you pass it.
- Post-repave, the exit is wider -- most cars now fit entirely on pavement to the right of the exit curb, so you have more room than you might expect.

### Turns 11-12 (Oak Tree) -- CRITICAL BRAKING ZONE

**The single most important braking zone on the circuit.** Oak Tree leads onto the 4,000-foot back straight. Every mph of exit speed here compounds over the entire straight.

- **Common mistake:** Braking too hard too early into Turn 11, then releasing the brakes and coasting between 11 and 12. This is the #1 time-loss error at VIR.
- **Correct technique:** Brake late into Turn 11 and maintain continuous brake pressure all the way through 11 into the Turn 12 entry. Think of it as ONE long braking zone, not two separate corners. "Once you apply the brakes, you don't want to get on the throttle until you arrive at the apex where the oak tree used to stand."
- **Brake pressure management:** Attack the initial brake zone going uphill, but get off *heavy* brake pressure early. The earlier you transition to lighter brake pressure, the more you can extend the brake zone without over-slowing.
- **Delay the final downshift:** Save your last downshift until about a car length before you are all the way left in the corner and turning back right. This helps roll more entry speed.
- **Throttle point:** Do not touch throttle until you reach the second apex (where the tree once stood) and are ready to open your hands. Once you pick up throttle, you should never have to lift again until end of the back straight.

**Source:** [Blayze -- Esses and Oak Tree at VIR](https://blayze.io/blog/car-racing/uphill-esses-and-oak-tree-at-vir), [Blayze -- Top 3 Mistakes](https://blayze.io/blog/car-racing/top-3-mistakes-at-virginia-international-raceway)

### Turn 13 (The Jog/Plunge) -- DECEPTIVELY SHORT BRAKING ZONE

- **The braking zone is extremely short despite the enormous speed at the end of the back straight.** The uphill grade provides massive supplemental braking force.
- Lower-power cars (you) often start braking at the 100-yard marker.
- **Common mistake:** Braking too hard too early, then getting off the brakes between Turns 13 and 14. Maintain braking pressure continuously from Turn 13 all the way to the turn-in for Turn 14.
- The compression at the bottom of the final dip before climbing up the hill helps slow the car. Use it.

### Turn 14 -- TRAIL BRAKE ZONE

- Right-hander cresting a hill. Benefits from trail braking due to the elevation change.
- Effectively a late-apex turn -- car should exit Turn 14 pointed to the right of Turn 15's apex.

### Turns 16-17 (Hog Pen) -- CRITICAL EXIT BRAKING ZONE

**Second most important corner on the circuit.** Hog Pen exit speed determines your speed for the entire front straight and into Turn 1.

- **Common mistake:** Early throttle application. Wait for the compression right at the apex on the right before getting on throttle.
- **Common mistake:** Not using full track width. Use "absolutely all of the curb on the left."
- **Correct technique:** Apply heaviest braking before the left curb, then lighten progressively. Eyes on the first left apex, using peripheral vision for the right curb turn-in. Delay throttle until you feel the compression at the right apex.
- The earlier you can get to full throttle in Turn 16b, the faster your lap time. For the Exige's 218 hp, every mph of exit speed here is amplified over the long front straight.

**Source:** [Blayze -- Top 3 Mistakes at VIR](https://blayze.io/blog/car-racing/top-3-mistakes-at-virginia-international-raceway)

## 2. Slow Corner Technique

### Oak Tree (Turns 11-12) -- THE MONEY CORNER

Three approaches exist, and the right one depends on your car:

1. **Smooth Arc** (recommended starting point): Miss the Turn 11 apex slightly, arrive parallel to the track on the left, maintenance throttle between 11 and 12, then commit to 12. Smoothest option.
2. **Short Straight**: Light braking, brief throttle, then brake again for Turn 12. Can work but introduces a throttle lift that costs time if not executed perfectly.
3. **Straight-line approach**: Carry maximum speed into 11, brake through the entire line to the Turn 12 entry. **Best for cars with stiff suspension and sticky tires (your Nitron/A7 combo).** Stiffer suspension and stickier tires have a higher likelihood of running this line successfully.

**For your car:** With Nitrons at 550/700 and Hoosier A7s, you should be able to run the straight-line approach (option 3). This is the fastest but demands precision.

**Key principle:** Use all the track to the left in the middle of the corner. VIR has added extra pavement beyond the traditional FIA exit curbs -- use all of it on exit. This track has been widened specifically to allow more aggressive exits.

**Where the time is:** "From the first esses through to Oak Tree is where most novices lose 5 seconds plus." If you are running 2:15, there are likely 1-2 seconds available in this section alone.

### Hog Pen (Turns 16-17)

Despite looking open on the map, Turn 16a is actually slow because of the setup required for Turn 16b. The sequence:

1. **Turn 16a:** Straighten your line on the right edge of the track between T15 and T16a. Take a late-apex entry. Do not track out much past the apex -- you need to be set up for 16b.
2. **Turn 16b:** This is where the time is made or lost. Get on throttle as early as possible. "The earlier the driver can get on throttle in turn 16b, the better their lap time will be."
3. **Turn 17:** For the Exige, this is a non-event. Low-power cars take it at full throttle. Keep your eyes on the exit curb to gauge speed. The bumpy pavement here can unsettle the car on stiff suspension -- "cars with stiffer suspensions may become momentarily airborne." Wait for the car to land, accept the oversteer rotation, and mash the throttle.

**Post-repave note:** The first right-hander of Hog Pen still has bumpy pavement. With your 550/700 springs and low ride height, the car may go momentarily light. The line doesn't change -- wait for the car to land and get on power.

### Climbing Esses (Turns 7-9)

**For your Exige (low HP, high grip):** Enter without braking, take under full power.

One LotusTalk member who went from 2:17 to 2:11 at VIR specifically cited going flatfoot through the esses as a key improvement: "I flatfooted through the esses on the first session on Thursday and she held so I started doing it and that helped towards the 2:11. Definitely one of those fear things but once you know the car can do it, it's fine."

**Critical safety rule:** Keep hands straight over every crest. "This is where we jump the car. Hands are straight, you land, over, just nibble that curb, get back to the right." Maintaining constant steering angle over any of the crests is dangerous and can cause a snap.

**Detailed sequence:**
- **Turn 7 (Left uphill):** Turn in from full track right under the bridge. Slightly late apex. Get the first right-hander done before the track falls away from you.
- **Turn 8a (Right):** Cut the entire inside curb -- right front tire partially hangs off the edge. Crest at the apex, then the track drops slightly.
- **Turn 8b (Left):** Crest at the apex again. Minimize lateral load going over the hump.
- **Turn 9 (Right):** More open than Turn 8. Partial to full throttle. Exit approximately track center to set up for South Bend.

**Progression tip:** Start with a late-apex line through each corner, hugging the left edge from apex to next turn-in. As you get faster, stop hugging the edge and just touch it at a single point between turns. Correct execution means the car crests each hill with barely any steering input.

**Source:** [Race & Track Driving -- VIR Full](https://racetrackdriving.com/track-guide/vir-full/), [Blayze -- Esses and Oak Tree](https://blayze.io/blog/car-racing/uphill-esses-and-oak-tree-at-vir)

### South Bend (Turn 10)

A very fast, downhill, blind left-hander that finishes in a steep downward slope.

- Turn in from extreme track right to maximize the curve radius.
- Look for the apex as soon as it physically appears in your line of sight (around the turn-in point).
- Post-repave, increased grip has made the corner less deceptive.
- For experienced drivers, the track-out point is the edge of the pavement a few feet past the end of the curb. Use the full width.
- **Setup dependency:** Getting South Bend right is the setup for Oak Tree. An early VIR expert said "the straight that ends at Oak Tree starts at Turn 4a" -- meaning everything from the Snake through the Esses through South Bend is all setup for the big money corner.

## 4. VIR-Specific Setup Considerations

### Elevation Changes and Their Impact

VIR has 130 feet (40m) of elevation change. This affects your Exige in specific ways:

**Braking zones:**
- **Turn 1 (downhill braking):** The downhill grade reduces braking effectiveness. Your AP Racing fronts with XP10 pads have strong initial bite, which helps, but the braking zone is inherently slippery here. Do not over-rely on initial brake force -- the ABS (if equipped) may activate earlier than expected.
- **Turn 13 (uphill braking):** The opposite -- the uphill provides massive supplemental braking force, making the braking zone deceptively short despite enormous entry speeds. Brake later than instinct suggests.

**Suspension loading:**
- The Climbing Esses create repeated compression-rebound cycles at high speed. Your 550/700 spring rates with Nitron 3-way dampers should handle this well, but the car will go light over every crest. This is where having hands straight matters.
- South Bend (Turn 10) drops away rapidly. The car unloads at the exit. With your stiff springs, the rear may go light momentarily.
- Hog Pen has bumpy pavement with an elevation drop. Cars on stiff suspensions "may become momentarily airborne." Your Nitron 3-ways should allow you to tune the compression damping to manage this.

### Tire Pressure Recommendations for VIR

Your car: ~2150 lbs with driver, Hoosier A7 205/45/16 front, 225/40/17 rear.

**Starting cold pressures for VIR:**
- Front: 26 psi cold
- Rear: 28 psi cold
- Target hot: 32-34 psi (front), 34-36 psi (rear)

**Minimum safe cold pressure:** 2150 / 100 = 21.5 psi (Hoosier formula for IRS cars with adequate camber). Your -3.0 front / -2.5 to -3.0 rear camber qualifies. However, Hoosier recommends never going below 26 psi cold on DOT radials.

**VIR surface characteristics:** Good grip, relatively abrasive. The A7s will heat up quickly, especially through the Esses and on the front straight under braking for Turn 1. Check pressures after 2-3 hot laps and bleed if above target. VIR in spring (March-May) offers excellent track conditions with moderate ambient temps.

**Dialing in:** Use your needle probe pyrometer. Take inside/middle/outside readings on all four tires immediately after hot laps (no cool-down lap). Target even temperatures across the tread.
- Inside hotter than outside: Too much negative camber
- Outside hotter than inside: Not enough negative camber
- Middle hotter than edges: Over-inflated, reduce 2 psi
- Middle cooler than edges: Under-inflated, add 2 psi

### Damper Settings for VIR

Your Nitron 3-way adjustables (currently all -8 compression, -10 rebound) allow VIR-specific tuning:

**For the Esses and elevation changes:**
- Consider slightly more compression damping (both high and low speed) to control the car over crests and through compressions. Going from -8 to -10 on compression may help maintain platform stability through Turns 7-10.
- The rebound setting at -10 is reasonable. If the car feels like it "pogo sticks" over crests, increase rebound damping slightly.

**For oversteer management (per your existing setup notes):**
- More front compression damping slows weight transfer off the front during corner entry
- More rear rebound damping slows how quickly the rear unloads during transitions

**A VIR-specific consideration:** The track rewards a car that is stable over crests and through compressions. The biggest risks are at the Esses crests and the Hog Pen bumps. Err toward slightly stiffer compression damping if in doubt. You can always back it off.

### Alignment Notes for VIR

Your existing alignment research (in Lotus.md) is thorough. VIR-specific notes:

- **Your -3.0 front camber** is excellent for VIR. The high-speed Esses and Oak Tree demand maximum front grip.
- **Rear camber at -2.5 to -2.7** (per your existing recommendation to reduce from -3.0) is appropriate for VIR. The high-speed direction changes in the Esses benefit from a wider rear contact patch.
- **Rear toe-in at 3-3.5mm** (per your existing recommendation to increase from 2mm) is particularly important at VIR. South Bend, the Esses, and Hog Pen all involve rapid direction changes and elevation-induced weight transfer that can unsettle the rear.
- **Your 0mm front toe** is correct for VIR. The long front straight rewards low drag, and turn-in is not the issue at VIR.

### Ride Height at VIR

Your current 130mm is safe for all of VIR. If you lower to the recommended 120/125mm:

- **Watch the compression at the bottom of the roller coaster** (between Oak Tree exit and the back straight). This is the lowest point on the circuit and where bottoming is most likely.
- **Watch the Hog Pen bumps.** Lower ride height + stiff springs + elevation drop = higher risk of bottoming.
- The diffuser will be more effective at lower ride heights, which matters most on the high-speed sections (front straight, back straight, Esses).

## 5. Section-by-Section Quick Reference

| Section | Key Technique | Common Mistake | Time Available |
|---------|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| **T1 Horseshoe** | Trail brake to apex, increasing radius so accelerate through second half | Lifting before brake zone, braking too early, turning in too early | 0.2-0.3s |
| **T2-3 NASCAR Bend** | Decreasing radius, late apex T3 | Over-cooking entry, running wide | 0.1-0.2s |
| **T4a** | Slowest corner at VIR, patience | Overdriving -- "the straight to Oak Tree starts here" | 0.1s |
| **T5-6 Snake** | Full throttle through, brief straighten between sub-corners | Turning in too early at 5a | 0.1-0.2s |
| **T7-9 Climbing Esses** | FLAT in your car, hands straight over crests | Lifting, maintaining steering angle over crests | 0.3-0.5s |
| **T10 South Bend** | Fast downhill blind left, full track width | Insufficient commitment, early lift | 0.1-0.2s |
| **T11-12 Oak Tree** | One long brake zone, throttle only at second apex | Braking too early, throttle too early, not using all track | 0.5-1.0s |
| **T13-14 Uphill** | Brake late (uphill helps), maintain pressure to T14 turn-in | Braking too early, releasing between 13-14 | 0.2-0.3s |
| **T15** | Full throttle in your car, accept some push | Over-slowing | 0.0-0.1s |
| **T16-17 Hog Pen** | Patience, late throttle, use all curbing, max exit speed | Early throttle, not using full track width | 0.3-0.5s |
